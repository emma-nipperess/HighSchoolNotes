➔ Equilibrium

Understand 3.1.1E: Understand that, over time, physical changes and reversible chemical reactions reach a state of dynamic equilibrium in a closed system, with the relative concentrations of products and reactants defining the position of equilibrium
  • In general, reversible reactions in a closed system eventually reach a situation where the rates of the forward and reverse reactions are equal. The system is described as reaching a state of equilibrium
  • At equilibrium, there still may be significant amounts of reactants in the system
  • *Equilibrium does not mean you have the same concentration of reactants and products  common mistake

Activation Energies

Explain 3.1.1F: Explain the reversibility of chemical reactions by considering the activation energies of the forward and reverse reactions

The reason as to why reverse reactions can occur, can be understood by referring to energy profile diagrams When particles collide, the energy associated with collisions can break bonds in reactant particles, allowing them to rearrange to form new products. The energy required to break reactant bonds in order for the reaction to occur  activation energy Is the forward reaction endothermic or exothermic? What about the reverse reaction? Provided that the reaction has enough energy to overcome the activation energy for both the forward and reverse reactions, the reaction proceeds until equilibrium is maintained.

Dynamic Equilibrium

  • Chemical equilibrium can be described as being in a dynamic state because the forward and reverse reactions have not stopped
  • During dynamic equilibrium:
    • Reaction is ‘incomplete’, and all substances are present in the system
    • At the molecular level, bonds are constantly being broken and new bonds are being formed as the reactants and products continue to convert into one another
    • The rate of the forward and reverse reactions become equal

    Components of the sample desorb off the stationary phase into the mobile phase if they are attracted to or have an affinity with the mobile phase.
